## 🎯 Project Objective

Knowledge without transfer has limited organizational value. Throughout my career, I have consistently delivered **training sessions on IFRS implementation and information security awareness** — helping finance and operations teams understand complex standards and security principles in **practical, actionable terms**. I provided first-time IFRS training to **20+ Banks and Financial Institutions** in Nepal — a landmark contribution during the country's transition from local GAAP to IFRS/NFRS.

> ⚠️ *Note: Institution-specific training materials, participant data, and internal content remain confidential. This project describes the training approach, topics, and delivery methodology.*

## 🛠️ Step-by-Step: What I Did

---

## PART A: IFRS/NFRS Training

---

### ✅ Step 1: Identified Training Need

- Recognized that Nepal's **first-time IFRS adoption** created a massive knowledge gap
- Most finance teams in BFIs had worked under local GAAP for decades
- Key challenges they faced:
  - unfamiliarity with IFRS concepts
  - difficulty understanding new measurement approaches (ECL, EIR, fair value)
  - confusion about accounting entries under new standards
  - anxiety about regulatory reporting changes
- Identified the need for **practical, banking-specific IFRS training** — not generic textbook sessions

---

### ✅ Step 2: Designed IFRS Training Content

- Designed training materials tailored specifically for **banking and finance professionals**:

#### 📋 Training Modules

| Module | Topics Covered |
|--------|---------------|
| **Module 1: IFRS Overview** | Why IFRS? GAAP vs. IFRS differences, first-time adoption (IFRS 1), transition timeline |
| **Module 2: Financial Instruments — IFRS 9** | Classification (AC/FVOCI/FVTPL), SPPI test, business model assessment, EIR method |
| **Module 3: Impairment — ECL Model** | 3-stage model, SICR, PD/LGD/EAD, forward-looking adjustment, worked ECL examples |
| **Module 4: Employee Benefits — IAS 19** | Defined benefit vs. defined contribution, actuarial valuation, OCI treatment, staff loan Day-1 loss |
| **Module 5: Income Tax — IAS 12** | Current tax, deferred tax, temporary differences, DTA/DTL recognition |
| **Module 6: Presentation — IAS 1** | Financial statement formats, OCI section, materiality, comparative information |
| **Module 7: Fair Value — IFRS 13** | Fair value hierarchy (Level 1, 2, 3), valuation techniques, investment measurement |
| **Module 8: Leases — IFRS 16** | ROU asset, lease liability, discount rate, short-term exemption |
| **Module 9: Disclosures — IFRS 7** | Credit risk, market risk, liquidity risk disclosures, sensitivity analysis |
| **Module 10: NRB Reconciliation** | IFRS provisions vs. NRB provisions, higher-of treatment, dual reporting |

- Each module included:
  - concept explanation in simple language
  - banking-specific worked examples
  - journal entry demonstrations
  - before vs. after IFRS comparison
  - common mistakes and how to avoid them
  - Q&A and discussion

---

### ✅ Step 3: Delivered IFRS Training Across 20+ BFIs

- Conducted training sessions at **20+ Banks and Financial Institutions** across Nepal
- Audience included:
  - Finance department staff
  - Branch accountants
  - Treasury officers
  - Operations and credit team members
  - Senior management and HODs
- Training formats used:
  - **Classroom sessions** — full-day or half-day workshops at client premises
  - **Hands-on workshops** — participants worked through actual IFRS adjustments with guidance
  - **Q&A sessions** — dedicated time for participants to ask questions about their specific situations
- Adapted training depth based on audience:
  - Senior management — strategic overview, key impacts, decision implications
  - Finance teams — detailed technical training with journal entries and computations
  - Operations teams — awareness-level training on how IFRS changes affect their areas

---

### ✅ Step 4: Provided Practical Transition Support

- Beyond formal training, provided **hands-on transition support** during IFRS adoption:
  - Walked finance teams through **actual IFRS adjustment entries** on their own data
  - Helped teams understand **how to read and prepare the new financial statement formats**
  - Guided teams on **ECL computation methodology** using their own loan data
  - Assisted with **chart of accounts migration** — mapping old accounts to new IFRS-aligned CoA
  - Answered ongoing questions as teams encountered real-world IFRS application issues
- This practical support was often more valuable than the classroom training — because teams could see IFRS applied to **their own numbers**

---

### ✅ Step 5: Created Lasting Reference Materials

- Prepared **reference materials** for participants to use after training:
  - Training slide decks
  - Worked example booklets
  - Journal entry quick-reference guides
  - IFRS disclosure checklists
  - FAQ documents addressing common IFRS questions in banking context
- These materials served as **ongoing reference** for finance teams during their first IFRS reporting cycles

## PART B: Information Security Awareness Training

---

### ✅ Step 6: Identified Information Security Training Need

- Recognized that most financial institutions had **limited staff awareness** of information security risks
- Common gaps observed:
  - weak password practices
  - clicking on suspicious links/emails
  - sharing credentials
  - using personal devices for official work without controls
  - lack of awareness about social engineering and phishing
  - poor understanding of data classification and handling
- Identified the need for **practical, non-technical security awareness training** that any staff member could understand and apply

---

### ✅ Step 7: Designed Information Security Training Content

- Designed training content tailored for **banking and financial sector staff**:

#### 📋 Training Modules

| Module | Topics Covered |
|--------|---------------|
| **Module 1: Why Security Matters** | Real-world examples of breaches in financial sector, impact on banks & customers, regulatory expectations |
| **Module 2: Password Security** | Strong password practices, password manager concept, multi-factor authentication (MFA) importance |
| **Module 3: Phishing & Social Engineering** | How phishing works, recognizing suspicious emails/links, vishing (voice phishing), smishing (SMS phishing), real examples |
| **Module 4: Email & Communication Security** | Secure email practices, attachment handling, verifying sender identity, reporting suspicious emails |
| **Module 5: Clean Desk & Physical Security** | Clear desk policy, screen lock, document handling, visitor management, restricted area access |
| **Module 6: Data Classification & Handling** | Public, internal, confidential, restricted data — how to handle each, sharing rules |
| **Module 7: Device Security** | Workstation security, USB usage, personal device risks, software update importance |
| **Module 8: Incident Reporting** | What constitutes a security incident, how to report, who to contact, importance of timely reporting |
| **Module 9: Regulatory Requirements** | NRB IT guidelines, data protection obligations, compliance expectations |
| **Module 10: Daily Security Habits** | Practical daily checklist — 10 things every staff member should do every day for security |

---

### ✅ Step 8: Delivered Security Awareness Sessions

- Conducted information security awareness sessions for:
  - Bank staff across departments
  - Finance teams handling sensitive financial data
  - IT team members (reinforcing security culture beyond technical controls)
  - Branch staff (physical security and customer data protection)
  - Management (governance and oversight responsibilities)
- Training approach:
  - **Interactive format** — not lecture-only; included quizzes, discussions, real-world case studies
  - **Live demonstrations** — showed how phishing emails look, how easy it is to guess weak passwords
  - **Scenario-based exercises** — "What would you do if..." scenarios to build practical judgment
  - **Non-technical language** — avoided jargon; made content accessible to all staff regardless of IT background

---

### ✅ Step 9: Connected Security Awareness to Audit Findings

- Linked security awareness training to **real audit observations** (anonymized):
  - shared generic examples of control failures caused by human error
  - explained how simple awareness could have prevented specific types of incidents
  - showed the connection between staff behavior and organizational security posture
- This made the training **directly relevant** — participants understood that security awareness wasn't theoretical but connected to actual risks in their workplace

---

### ✅ Step 10: Measured Training Effectiveness

- Assessed training effectiveness through:

#### 📋 Training Evaluation Framework

| Metric | Measurement Approach |
|--------|---------------------|
| **Knowledge Improvement** | Pre-training quiz vs. post-training quiz — average score increase |
| **Participant Satisfaction** | Anonymous feedback forms rating content quality, relevance, and delivery (1-5 scale) |
| **Engagement Level** | Number of questions asked during session; participation in exercises |
| **Practical Application** | Participants' self-reported intention to change specific behaviors; follow-up surveys |

- Tracked improvement metrics:
Example Results:
Average knowledge score: Pre-training 45% → Post-training 82% (+37 points)
Average satisfaction rating: 4.6/5.0
Engagement: Average 12 questions asked per session
Behavioral intent: 94% of participants committed to applying at least 3 security practices


- Used feedback to **continuously improve** training content and delivery for future sessions:
  - Updated phishing examples based on emerging threats
  - Added more banking-specific scenarios based on participant requests
  - Refined quiz questions to better assess practical understanding
